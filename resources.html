Coronaviruses are a large family of viruses which may cause illness in animals or humans. In humans, several coronaviruses are known to cause respiratory infections ranging from the common cold to more severe diseases such as Middle East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS) and Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS). The most recently discovered coronavirus causes coronavirus disease COVID-19.

Source: World Health Organization

Symptoms range from mild to severe and can include:

  • fever
  • cough
  • difficulty breathing
  • muscle aches
  • fatigue
  • headache
  • sore throat
  • runny nose

Most people with mild coronavirus illness will recover on their own.

Source: Ontario Ministry of Health

Human coronaviruses cause infections of the nose, throat and lungs. They are most commonly spread from an infected person through:

  • respiratory droplets generated when you cough or sneeze
  • close, prolonged personal contact, such as touching or shaking hands
  • touching something with the virus on it, then touching your mouth, nose or eyes before washing your hands

Source: Public Health Agency of Canada

There is no vaccine available to protect against the 2019 novel coronavirus, but the following everyday actions can help reduce exposure to the virus:
  • stay at home as much as possible
  • avoid all non-essential trips in your community
  • if you leave your home, keep a distance of at least 2 metres from others
  • wash your hands often with soap and water for at least 20 seconds
  • avoid touching your eyes, nose, or mouth with unwashed hands
  • cough or sneeze into a tissue or the bend of your arm, not your hand
  • clean frequently touch surfaces such as toilets, door handles, phones, etc. with the appropriate disinfectant
  • consider wearing a non-medical mask in public spaces
Source: Public Health Agency of Canada

At this time, there is no vaccine or therapy to treat or prevent this disease. Currently, the treatment of COVID-19 includes supportive care and treatment of any secondary infections, such as pneumonia. Nevertheless, clinical trials for COVID-19 vaccines are ongoing around the world including many in Canada.

Source: Health Canada

As of April 24, hundreds of studies for COVID-19 have been authorized worldwide including 18 in Canada. Some notable news with respect to clinical trials:

April 24: University of Oxford starts recruiting for its study of a candidate vaccine, ChAdOx1 nCoV-19, in UK.

April 22: Pfizer and BioNTech announced that Germany has approved the Phase 1/2 clinical trial of BioNTech's BNT162 vaccine program.

April 10: Gilead reported the outcomes of 53 patients who received remdesivir on a compassionate basis.

April 06: Inovio Pharmaceuticals has initiated phase I clinical trial of INO-4800, its DNA vaccine for COVID-19.

April 06: Genentech enrolls first patients in a trial testing arthritis drug Actemra against COVID-19.

April 01: Hospitals across Canada join WHO's Solidarity Trial to test lopinavir/ritonavir against COVID-19.

March 29: McGill University Health Centre in Montreal are launching an innovative trial to assess the drug hydroxychloroquine.

March 17: Pfizer and BioNTech announced that they will be co-developing a potential mRNA-based coronavirus vaccine, which is expected to enter clinical testing by the end of April 2020.

March 16: Sanofi and Regeneron started a clinical program evaluating Kevzara (sarilumab) in patients hospitalized with severe COVID-19 infection.

March 12: AbCellera, a Vancouver company and Eli Lilly announced that they will co-develop antibody products for treatment and prevention of COVID-19.

Note that there are many, many trials recruiting and in progress at the moment. The above list of trials is by no means the most promising but merely the ones I found notable.
